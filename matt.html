Personal Reflection

The aim of this reflective report is to reflect on the usability study we conducted as a whole and to allow students to critically think about the process we have undertaken, discus what we have learnt and what we will be able to use in the future of our careers. Reflection is vital to understanding the why and how things have happened and allows us to adjust our views and actions to positively impact the future. By leveraging this information and learning from our mistakes we are able to make positive change and improve our workflow. Currently, I am in my final weeks studying a Bachelor of Computer and Information Sciences at AUT where I major in Software Development. My current (relative) work experience consists of working over the summer at a medium sized software company called Serko located in Parnell where I worked as an intern. I was working with a team of 6 others in developing an internal human resources application aimed at synchronising the data from two external providers through the use of API’s. This has given me vital real world experience in a real fast moving company as well as working in a team that is completely new together. This experience helped me grow and thrive as an individual and really gave me a step up in the software development game by improving my development capabilities as well as my confidence talking to important stakeholders. It has also shown me that problems can easily arise in group environments; especially when there are disagreements with the direction of a certain product.

While working on this assignment, we stayed in our original groups from the first half of the paper. The team works well together and we are able to be transparent about what we are thinking; this allows us to solve problems quickly and effectively as they arise rather than letting issues fester to the detriment of the team. Generally, I find that working in a team can be a lot of work as there is overhead to find out what everyone is comfortable doing and what everyone is good at. Sometimes it’s much easier when you know you need to do and you can just go ahead and get started at any stage without needing the approval of other team members. I have also found in my whole experience at AUT that working in a team means you need to compromise on certain components such as quality or project direction. For example, if documents or code are not to the standard you would normally produce you can’t just re-write what your teammate has written and completely change something because it suits. Saying this, our team has been working very well together and produced work of a high standard that everyone can be proud of. Our communication has also been great and everyone has been active in the Facebook chat we created which has positively helped the group dynamic. When we started this project, we were quite lost in terms of the direction we wanted to go and what question we wanted to answer. We first started brainstorming ideas and came up with some an around FPS shooters and ‘if playing more hours a day means an improvement in hand-eye coordination’ but after some more discussions we felt like this was a bad idea and would be pointless. Essentially, we were going to ask if FPS shooter games would mean youre better at clicking on moving circles on a computer screen; which I think we all knew the answer already and would have made the whole exercise trivial and unimportant.

During the assignment I worked on various aspects of the project. I conducted all the interviews with the participants where I took them through what we needed them to do, which includes getting them setup and asking interview questions at the end. I aided in setting up the preliminary scenario and finding the best way to test the application. I was also responsible for the methodology section of the website, which defines what we did and how we went about completing the study which I worked on with Yash. Even though it wasn't specified or required I also acted as a pseudo leader for the group where I helped guide team members in their tasks and provide feedback when necessary.

Previously when showing the application to people I would tell them what it does and how to use it without thinking that there are any issues with it. This way of showing people has clouded the true usability of the website; as I have shown people how to use everything rather than just letting them try it out and finding the flaws. In conclusion, before conducting this project, I didn’t understand the importance of getting users who have never seen the application before to try it out while actively watching what they do without guidance. After conducting a usability study of our own, it has been made apparent that what I might think is an obvious feature that no one can miss is really not. Different people have different expectations and backgrounds, and what is obvious to me is not obvious to everyone else. Being able to create a truly intuitive application that doesn't require a help section requires us to look outside the box and into other people's perspective. From now on, whenever I will be working in the industry or on my own projects I will ensure that I conduct some sort of qualitative usability study, even if it's just on my own family or friends. Hopefully, these findings from the usability study will help the development of a more intuitive AUT application that students can use and be proud of.
